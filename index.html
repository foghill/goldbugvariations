Synopsis

1957. Stuart Ressler arrives at Illinois on a fellowship to crack the four-letter alphabet of life. By 1985 he is loading magnetic tapes on a Brooklyn graveyard shift, anonymous, brilliant, and unaccountably lost.

When research librarian Jan O'Deigh receives a single reference question about Ressler's vanished career, she begins an investigation that rhymes — in fugue and counterpoint — with the work he abandoned.

A four-handed study of two romances divided by a quarter century, structured like a Bach fugue: thirty variations on a single, recursive theme.

FOUR VOICES, TWO CENTURIES

The pairing is not chronological. It is musical: a question in 1957 is answered, fifteen years of plot later, by a gesture in 1985. The audience learns to listen for the rhyme.

The third act is the moment the two voices land on the same chord — the screen becomes one image, briefly, before parting again.

WHY IT'S ON THE DESK

The film's two strands — the Goldberg Variations and the four bases of DNA — are both arithmetic that does not feel like arithmetic. Leibniz heard it three centuries early.
